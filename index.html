Retrieval signal fusion
01

When more retrieval signals make results worse

Search systems often combine several signals — keyword matching, semantic similarity, and metadata — to rank results. Combining them isn't automatic: the scores aren't on the same scale, keyword-heavy documents can crowd out better semantic matches, and a fixed limit on candidates can push the right answer out before it's even ranked. The fix is careful fusion — routing between retrieval modes based on the query, calibrating thresholds, and reranking with a clear reason for combining each signal.

Structured & graph-aware retrieval
02

Why hard domain questions expose RAG weaknesses

In regulated or technical domains, similarity search alone isn't enough — two passages can look nearly identical to an embedding model while meaning something different. Questions like these need structured facts, consistent terminology, and the ability to pull in related sections or hop across a knowledge graph, not just a better embedding model. Retrieval systems that hold up in these domains treat structure as part of the design, not something bolted on afterward.

Content gap analysis
03

When the answer is missing, not wrong

A low evaluation score doesn't always mean retrieval failed — sometimes the answer just isn't in the knowledge base yet. Working closely with subject matter experts, I used evaluation signals to spot these content gaps, then fed them back into what needed to be added or rewritten. That turns evaluation from a one-time score into a feedback loop that keeps improving the system over time.
